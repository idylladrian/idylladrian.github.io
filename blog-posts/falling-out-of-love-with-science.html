Falling Out of Love with Science

From Mimipedia, sourced from my brain (and references)

Getting Lost

Drawing of two sad robots,
generated by Dall-E

Originally Published:

July 16, 2023

“I am a little scientist, in constant awe and amazement of the universe; running around and exploring the forest in my backyard with my pocket-magnifying glass,” I wrote in my college application essay.

“Ah yes,” I thought to myself. “A small, explicative description of my youth; painting myself as having been a naturally curious and astute researcher since I was able to walk. Just like the unnaturally-young honesty of Abraham Lincoln or intelligence of Sandra Day O’Connor. They will have no choice but to accept my application because I cannot make my inherent talent more clear.

“I was lying of course. Did I enjoy running around and playing outside? Yes. Did I do it with a magnifying glass and a hypothesis in my head? No. When I reflected on the qualities that I expected in a scientist, and compared them to myself: I did not see them. There was a disconnect between my small-town upbringing and the superbly amazing life stories of the scientists I wanted to embody from television and video games. When I got access to the internet, I spent hours googling the accomplishments of high-achievers that were my age to gauge my distance from the scientist identity I hoped I would embody someday. My favorite group of gifted kids to investigate were winners of international junior science competitions. I would lay in bed wondering why I, at a near decrepit age of sixteen, had not sequenced slug DNA, like that seventh-grader from Massachusetts. Perhaps it was my lack of drive. My disappointingly-average aptitude for the sciences and math. No. It was probably because I was assigned too many chores. Yes. I could not reach my full potential because I had to wash the dinner dishes twice a week.

Now that I work as a scientist, I know that the occupation of a ‘scientist’ is not as cutthroat as I had manufactured it to be. But in high school, all I could think to do was lie on my applications. If I didn’t feel that I had the behaviors and affinities of a true scientist in the making, then I would at least tell them that I did. And it worked.

The first time I credibly questioned the whole scientist-is-the-coolest-job- ever-thing was when I got into upper-level classes in college. After two years of taking classes that leaders in academia meticulously designed to remind me that I was stupid, I had reached the level of education where I could begin to specialize in my study. I had been promised by upper-classmen that “when you take the junior and senior-level courses, is when the cool stuff starts.” My feigned enthusiasm for science that brought me to the university had been exhausted because the advanced classes were also boring. Having fully engaged with science in all ways that I was told were fun and realistic, I wondered what was wrong. With more introspection and therapy, I deduced that my desire to be a scientist was actually a façade for a simpler desire: I wished to push colorful buttons and work on the Starship Enterprise.

Unfortunately, it was quickly becoming clear to me that becoming a scientist would not help me achieve my dream. Not because I was lacking anything, but because no one, in fact, would be traveling faster-than-light on a super-cool, imaginary spaceship. At least, not anytime soon.

I settled for environmental engineering as my area of focus. If I couldn’t run away to be an astronaut in the way I wanted, I would at least be doing something for the planet. It was around this time, that I also became really involved in learning and working with the decolonization groups on our campus. A few close friends and I would regularly chat and work to further understand STEM through the lens of colonialism and how its framework enabled violence. The last shreds of hope I had for enthusiasm in science shriveled up as it became impossible not to question and critique every component of my learning. If I was learning engineering to heal the environment, then why are we engaging in fossil fuels as a legitimate option for the energy of the future in our lectures? I questioned professors why class is apolitical and capitalistic when climate change is anything but apolitical and is exacerbated by capitalism. Why are the work and knowledge of indigenous peoples, who are nearly the sole protectors of biodiversity and land, treated as inferior to the understandings of white men? The excitement and intrigue left me quickly in my academic journey, but by the end, so had my respect for the fields in their entirety.

After graduating from college, I work as an environmental scientist at an environmental consulting firm (evil companies hire us to clean up their environmental dumping after they get sued). I was very excited about my job when I started my internship. Even if I disagreed with the theory of science and how it is performed, the praxis of cleaning the environment would no doubt be a net positive that would nurture my desire to do good. SIKE! Please see my official statement below on my job:

“After an extensive career (one year of interning, one year of full-time work) of assisting in the development of plans for environmental cleanup, witnessing the crippling inefficiencies of bureaucracy that actually is designed to support the offending polluters of our bodies of water, and being forced to work with white, cishet, neurotypical “humans”; I advise that we all collectively quit our jobs and maybe stop taking science so seriously because it’s a pretentious joke.“

P.S. We should still take vaccines seriously.

Vaccines aren’t a joke. Now I sit, almost 23, writing a blog post in a café, thinking to myself “What’s next? because all of this seems silly”. I spent nearly my entire life wanting to become a scientist. Now that I am one, I can feel this shape that I’ve massaged myself into, losing shape. A paper-mâché cast of an identity that I was saving in the back, waiting for when I could finally slip into because it would fit perfectly; it has disintegrated back into the paper.
